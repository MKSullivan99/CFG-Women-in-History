Marie Curie

Physicist and Chemist

Marie Curie emerged as a pioneering figure in the realm of science, notably distinguishing herself as the first woman to receive a Nobel Prize. Her achievements are underscored by the fact that she is the only individual to have been awarded Nobel Prizes in two distinct scientific disciplines. Curie's relentless pursuit of knowledge led her to isolate two new elements, radium and polonium, earning her a second Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1911. Curie's legacy endures, not only through her scientific achievements but also as a symbol of perseverance and dedication in the face of societal constraints, inspiring generations of scientists to follow in her footsteps.

Florence Nightingale

Founder of modern nursing
Click to read her bio

Florence Nightingale, born on 12 May 1820 into a wealthy British family, defied the expectations of her time to forge a new path in nursing. She became a key figure during the Crimean War, where her implementation of strict sanitation practices drastically reduced the death rate among soldiers, laying the foundations for modern nursing. Nightingale's advocacy for cleanliness, nutrition, and compassionate care revolutionised hospital conditions and established nursing as a respected profession. Her statistical skills demonstrated the impact of sanitary practices on health outcomes, leading to widespread healthcare reforms. After the war, she founded the Nightingale School of Nursing in London, promoting the education and formal training of nurses. Her seminal work, "Notes on Nursing," remains a cornerstone of nursing education. Nightingale's enduring legacy reflects her visionary approach and dedication, making her an enduring symbol of nursing and healthcare reform.

Dr. Quarraisha Abdool Karim

Epidemiologist
Click to read her bio

Dr. Quarraisha Abdool Karim is a distinguished South African epidemiologist and researcher, renowned for her pivotal work in the fight against HIV/AIDS. With a career spanning several decades, she has focused on understanding the HIV epidemic in sub-Saharan Africa, particularly its impact on women and girls. Her research has significantly contributed to the development of HIV prevention strategies, including the groundbreaking use of antiretroviral drugs to prevent transmission. Alongside her husband, scientist Salim S. Abdool Karim, she has led several influential studies that have shaped global policies on HIV prevention and treatment. As an advocate for women's health and rights, she continues to influence the scientific community and policy-making, aiming to reduce the spread of HIV and improve the lives of those affected by the virus. Her work exemplifies a commitment to excellence, compassion, and innovation in addressing one of the most challenging health crises of our time.

Rosalind Franklin

Description
Click to read her bio

Rosalind Franklin, an English chemist born on 25 July 1920, played a pivotal role in uncovering the structure of DNA with her expertise in X-ray crystallography. Her photograph 51, an X-ray diffraction image, was crucial for identifying DNA's double helix structure. Despite her significant contributions, Franklin's work was not fully recognized during her lifetime, and she did not share in the Nobel Prize later awarded to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins for the discovery of the DNA structure. Franklin also made notable strides in virology and the study of coal. She passed away prematurely on 16 April 1958, leaving behind a legacy that continues to inspire and impact the scientific community.
