Women in Science

Marie Curie

French Physicist
Click to read her bio

Marie Curie, along with her husband Pierre, discovered polonium and radium in 1898. Her contributions to physics were immense, not only in her own work, as indicated by her two Nobel Prizes, but also through her influence on subsequent generations of nuclear physicists and chemists. Her work paved the way for the discovery of the neutron and artificial radioactivity.

Rosalind Franklin

British Chemist
Click to read her bio

Rosalind Franklin discovered the density of DNA and established that the molecule existed in a helical conformation. Her work to make clearer X-ray patterns of DNA molecules laid the foundation for Watson and Crick’s suggestion that DNA is a double-helix polymer in 1953. Franklin also contributed new insight on the structure of viruses, opening the way for structural virology.

Alice Augusta Ball

American Chemist
Click to read her bio

Alice Augusta Ball was the first woman to graduate from the College of Hawaii in 1915 with a Master’s degree in Chemistry. She was the first African American research chemist and instructor in the college’s chemistry department. Alice Ball was also the first person to successfully develop a water-soluble, injectable form of chaulmoogra oil that was used for decades to treat Hansen’s disease.

Dorothy Hodgkin

X-Ray Pioneer
Click to read her bio

Dorothy Hodgkin was awarded the 1964 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for solving the atomic structure of molecules such as penicillin and insulin, using X-ray crystallography. She remains the only British woman to be awarded a Nobel Prize for science. This came in 1964, when she became the sole recipient of the Prize for chemistry ‘for her determinations by X-ray techniques of the structures of important biochemical substances’.
